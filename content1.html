Background

The portrayal of Latin American drug cartels in mainstream media, from hit Netflix dramas such as Narcos to blockbuster video games like GTA series and other crime related games, powerfully shapes how the public views Latin America. The names and stories of infamous drug lords like Pablo Escobar and Joaquin "El Chapo" Guzman are told and recognized like celebrities and star atheletes. In these media outlets,the violent kingpins and narcos are cast as central characters or even resepcted figures in Latin America, and their home countries are depicted often through a distorted lens that emphasizes crime and chaos above all else. Cultural theorist Stuart Hall noted that stereotyping reduces people to a few simple, fixed traits and sets up a symbolic frontier between “us” and “them” based on the "West" and the "Rest" (Hall, 1996). In the case of cartel portrayals, Latin Americans are frequently the “Other”, violent traffickers or corrupt officials, while American or foreign agents appear as saviors to defeat their plan of poisoning Americans. This report explores how shows like Narcos and games like GTA construct these stereotypes, how a narcocultura of glorified drug lords has emerged, and how such images feed into U.S. political discourse on crime and immigration regarding Latinos. For example. the recent mass deportation of Hispanic immigrants in U.S. is justified as "expelling criminals" by the administration. In the process, we will connect these trends to Hall’s West and the Rest discourse and Quijano’s Coloniality of Power to understand the impact of these media narratives and the need to challenge the “single story” of Latin America as just a land of cartels and violence (Brown, 2015)
