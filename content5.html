West and the Rest

Stuart Hall

Stuart Hall (1932–2014) was born in Kingston, Jamaica, into a mixed-race, middle-class family. He won a Rhodes Scholarship to Oxford in 1951 and moved to the UK, where he became one of the founding figures of the Centre for Contemporary Cultural Studies at the University of Birmingham. Hall’s work pioneered “cultural studies,” arguing that culture is a site of power struggle and identity formation rather than a free-floating realm of “taste.” His key publications include Representation (1997) and Cultural Studies 1983: A Theoretical History. He remained a public intellectual and a leading voice on race, identity, and globalization until his death in 2014.

Connecting the Dots: Cartel Narratives & West and the Rest Discourse

Stuart Hall’s essay “The West and the Rest: Discourse and Power” in 1996 meticulously examines the ways in which Western societies have historically constructed and disseminated narratives that position themselves as the arbiters of progress, civilization, and modernity, while simultaneously depicting non-Western cultures, particularly those in Latin America, as backward, chaotic, or inherently criminal. Following Hall's demonstration, we can unpack how these representations serve to reinforce culutre hieracrchies and legitimize foreign and domestic policies.

Discursive Formation

Hall’s concept of discursive formation refers to the structured ways in which knowledge and meaning are organized and circulated across media channels. When shows like Narcos and dozens of Spanish-language “narconovelas” prioritize storylines about drug trafficking, they cumulatively forge a collective imaginary that associates Hispanic ethnicity with criminal syndicates. This repeated framing not only shapes entertainment content but also seeps into mainstream news reporting, academic discourse, and everyday conversations, thereby cementing a stereotype that becomes difficult to dislodge.

Regime of Truth

The “regime of truth” in Hall’s framework describes the institutions and practices that validate certain narratives as authoritative and factual. In the context of cartel coverage, networks and streaming platforms assume the role of truth-makers by emphasizing purportedly “insider” perspectives, dramatized reenactments, and commentary from law-enforcement officials. This veneer of authenticity leads audiences to accept depictions of Hispanic communities through the narrow lens of narco violence, often without questioning how these stories are selected, produced, and edited to serve broader geopolitical or commercial aims.

Knowledge and Power

Knowledge production is never neutral as those who control the creation and dissemination of information hold significant power. Political actors and media conglomerates leverage cartel- focused narratives to justify policies such as enhanced border security, mass deportations, and expanded surveillance in Hispanic neighborhoods. By framing these measures as necessary responses to an existential threat posed by narco networks, they harness fear to consolidate support for legislation that deliberately target Hispanic individuals, often undermining due-process rights and hurting trust in legal institutions.

Discursive Strategies

Through tactics like stereotyping, splitting, and pathologizing, media narratives reduce complex social phenomena to simplistic binaries: innocent migrant vs. ruthless cartel hitman; law-abiding citizen vs. criminal “other.” Such discursive strategies facilitate the erasure of diverse Hispanic voices and experiences, granting political leaders the ability to portray entire communities as monolithic threats. In turn, this paves the way for policies that target Hispanic populations collectively, from aggressive immigration raids to voter suppression efforts in Latino precincts.
